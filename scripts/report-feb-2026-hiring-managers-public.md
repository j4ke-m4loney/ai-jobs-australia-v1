# Hiring AI Talent in Australia: What the Data Says — February 2026

*Published: February 2026 | AI Jobs Australia Employer Report*

If you're hiring for AI, ML, or Data Science roles in Australia, here's what you're up against — and how to stand out. This report is drawn from real hiring activity and job seeker behaviour on AI Jobs Australia.

---

## The Market at a Glance

The AI job market in Australia is competitive and accelerating. In the first half of February alone, we saw thousands of job searches but only a **16% search-to-apply rate**. Candidates are browsing heavily but being highly selective about where they apply.

Your listing needs to stand out.

---

## 1. Your Competition Is Growing

New AI roles are being posted at a faster rate than January, with over 120 companies actively hiring. Financial services is leading the charge — the big four banks and major insurers collectively account for over 10% of all listings. Tech companies and consulting firms round out the top employers.

If you're competing for the same talent pool, you're up against well-resourced, well-known brands.

---

## 2. What Candidates Are Searching For

The most searched terms tell you exactly what talent is looking for:

- **"AI Engineer"** — the most searched job title
- **"Data Scientist"** and **"Data Science"** — consistently high volume
- **"Machine Learning"** — strong and steady demand
- **"Junior"** and **"Entry Level"** — a growing segment

**Action:** Make sure your job title includes these keywords. A listing titled "Technical Specialist - Advanced Analytics" will get fewer views than "Data Scientist" or "ML Engineer". Candidates search for the role, not your internal job title.

Several major employers are also being searched for by name — if your brand isn't one of them, your job listing is doing the heavy lifting alone.

---

## 3. Salary: The Elephant in the Room

**Under 15% of AI job listings disclose salary.** That means over 85% of employers are asking candidates to apply blind.

Here's why that's a problem: candidates are increasingly filtering and self-selecting based on salary expectations. If you're not disclosing, you're losing applicants to those who are.

### What the Market Is Paying

Among roles that do disclose salary:

| Category | Avg Midpoint |
|----------|-------------|
| Machine Learning | Highest — ~35% above overall average |
| AI Engineering | Above average |
| Software Development | Above average |
| Data Engineering | At average |
| Strategy & Transformation | Slightly below average |
| AI Governance | Below average |
| Data Science | Lowest of the technical roles |

ML specialists command the highest salaries — significantly above the overall market average. If you're offering below market, you'll struggle to attract quality candidates. If you're offering at or above, **say so in the listing** — it's your strongest competitive advantage.

---

## 4. The Remote Work Gap

This is the biggest mismatch in the data:

- **~25% of candidates specifically filter for remote work**
- **Under 10% of jobs offer it**
- **~56% of jobs are hybrid**, making hybrid the default — not a differentiator

If your role can be done remotely, advertising it as such immediately puts you in a less competitive pool. Offering hybrid alone doesn't help you stand out — but offering **genuine remote flexibility** could be a significant talent magnet.

Remote flexibility varies by category:
- **Machine Learning roles** are the most flexible — about a third are fully remote
- **Data Science roles** skew the opposite way — the majority are onsite
- **AI Governance and Product roles** are almost entirely hybrid

---

## 5. Where the Candidates Are

- **~70% of AI job seekers are in Victoria or New South Wales**
- Queensland, ACT, and other states make up a much smaller share
- Remote is the third most popular search filter after VIC and NSW

If you're hiring outside the two major states, expect a smaller local talent pool — but significantly less competition for attention.

---

## 6. What Makes Candidates Click "Apply"

Analysing the most applied-to roles this month, clear patterns emerge:

**Listings that attract the most clicks share these traits:**
- Clear, searchable job titles ("AI Engineer" not "Technical Specialist")
- "Remote" or multi-city options listed prominently
- Mission-driven or AI-native employers
- Entry-level opportunities (massively undersupplied relative to demand)

The top-performing listing this month received more than double the apply clicks of any other — it had a specific, clear title, a focused role description, and a recognisable employer brand.

---

## 7. How Technical Is the Hiring Bar?

Our AI analysis of interview difficulty across roles posted this month:

| Difficulty | Share |
|------------|-------|
| Easy | ~3% |
| Medium | ~30% |
| Hard | ~58% |
| Very Hard | ~10% |

**Two-thirds of AI roles have hard or very hard interview processes.** If your hiring process is technically demanding, you're the norm — but be aware that candidates have multiple options. Slow, multi-round processes lose candidates to faster-moving competitors.

---

## 8. Career Growth Matters to Candidates

Our analysis also looked at career progression signals across listings:

- **~97% of roles show medium or high promotion potential** — candidates expect growth
- **~28% of roles offer high autonomy** — appealing for self-directed engineers
- **Zero roles scored low on autonomy** this month

If your listing doesn't signal career growth or autonomy, you're at a disadvantage against employers that do. Mention progression paths, learning budgets, or leadership opportunities in your job description.

---

## 9. The Opportunity for Employers

Based on this month's data, here are the highest-leverage moves:

### Disclose your salary
Under 15% of listings show salary. Candidates increasingly expect it, and listings with salary information convert better. You don't need to give an exact number — a range works.

### Offer remote or genuine hybrid
A quarter of candidates want remote, but under 10% of jobs offer it. Even partial remote flexibility puts you ahead of 90% of the market.

### Use searchable job titles
"AI Engineer", "Data Scientist", "Machine Learning Engineer" — these are what candidates type into the search bar. Don't bury the role behind corporate jargon.

### Don't overlook junior talent
"Junior" and "entry level" are among the most common search terms, yet almost no entry-level roles were posted this month. There's an undersupplied segment of eager candidates ready to be developed.

### Move fast
Candidates have hundreds of active listings to choose from. The average AI role is hard to interview for — if your process takes 6+ weeks, you're likely losing finalists to faster offers.
